## 1. Mathematical Foundations

Quantum computing relies heavily on linear algebra, complex numbers, and probability theory. Mastery of these fundamentals is essential.

### 1.1 Complex Numbers

**Definition**: A complex number is expressed as $z = a + bi$ where $a,b \in \mathbb{R}$ and $i^2 = -1$.

**Polar Form**: $z = re^{i\theta}$ where:
- $r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ (modulus/magnitude)
- $\theta = \arg(z) = \tan^{-1}(b/a)$ (argument/phase)

**Euler's Formula**: $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$

**Complex Conjugate**: $\bar{z} = a - bi$

**Key Properties**:
- $z\bar{z} = |z|^2$
- $\overline{z_1 + z_2} = \bar{z}_1 + \bar{z}_2$
- $\overline{z_1 z_2} = \bar{z}_1 \bar{z}_2$
- $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|$
- $\arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2)$

**Example**: For $z = 3 + 4i$:
- $|z| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5$
- $\arg(z) = \tan^{-1}(4/3) \approx 0.927$ radians
- $z^{-1} = \frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^2} = \frac{3-4i}{25} = 0.12 - 0.16i$

### 1.2 Vector Spaces and Linear Algebra

**Vector Space**: A set $V$ over field $\mathbb{F}$ (here $\mathbb{C}$) with operations:
- Vector addition: $+: V \times V \rightarrow V$
- Scalar multiplication: $\cdot: \mathbb{F} \times V \rightarrow V$
Satisfying 8 axioms (associativity, commutativity, identity, inverses, distributivity).

**Basis**: A set of linearly independent vectors that span the space.
**Dimension**: Number of vectors in any basis.

**Inner Product Space**: Vector space with inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$:
- Conjugate symmetric: $\langle u, v \rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle}$
- Linear in second argument: $\langle u, av + bw \rangle = a\langle u, v \rangle + b\langle u, w \rangle$
- Positive definite: $\langle v, v \rangle \geq 0$ with equality iff $v = 0$

**Norm**: $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$

**Orthogonality**: $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$

**Orthonormal Set**: $\langle v_i, v_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$ (Kronecker delta)

### 1.3 Matrices and Operators

**Matrix Representation**: Linear map $T: V \rightarrow W$ represented by matrix $[T]_{ij}$ where $T(v_j) = \sum_i [T]_{ij} w_i$.

**Matrix Multiplication**: $(AB)_{ik} = \sum_j A_{ij} B_{jk}$

**Special Matrices**:
- **Identity**: $I_{ij} = \delta_{ij}$
- **Zero**: $0_{ij} = 0$
- **Diagonal**: $D_{ij} = 0$ for $i \neq j$
- **Hermitian**: $A^\dagger = A$ where $A^\dagger = \bar{A}^T$ (conjugate transpose)
- **Unitary**: $U^\dagger U = I$ (preserves inner product)
- **Projector**: $P^2 = P$ and $P^\dagger = P$

**Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors**:
For matrix $A$, scalar $\lambda$ and nonzero vector $v$ are eigenvalue/eigenvector pair if:
$Av = \lambda v$

**Characteristic Equation**: $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$

**Spectral Theorem** (Finite-Dimensional):
Every Hermitian matrix $H$ can be diagonalized as:
$H = UDU^\dagger$
where $U$ is unitary and $D$ is diagonal with real entries (eigenvalues).

**For quantum mechanics**: Observables correspond to Hermitian operators; measurement yields eigenvalues.

### 1.4 Tensor Product

**Definition**: For vector spaces $V$ and $W$, the tensor product $V \otimes W$ is generated by symbols $v \otimes w$ subject to bilinearity:
- $(v_1 + v_2) \otimes w = v_1 \otimes w + v_2 \otimes w$
- $v \otimes (w_1 + w_2) = v \otimes w_1 + v \otimes w_2$
- $(\alpha v) \otimes w = v \otimes (\alpha w) = \alpha(v \otimes w)$

**Basis**: If $\{v_i\}$ basis of $V$ and $\{w_j\}$ basis of $W$, then $\{v_i \otimes w_j\}$ is basis of $V \otimes W$.

**Dimension**: $\dim(V \otimes W) = (\dim V)(\dim W)$

**Action on Product States**: 
$(A \otimes B)(v \otimes w) = (Av) \otimes (Bw)$

**Mixed-Product Property**: 
$(A \otimes B)(C \otimes D) = (AC) \otimes (BD)$

**Entanglement**: A state in $V \otimes W$ is entangled if it cannot be written as $v \otimes w$.

### 1.5 Probability Theory

**Sample Space**: Set $\Omega$ of all possible outcomes.

**Event**: Subset $E \subseteq \Omega$.

**Probability Measure**: Function $P: \mathcal{F} \rightarrow [0,1]$ where $\mathcal{F}$ is $\sigma$-algebra of events, satisfying:
1. $P(\Omega) = 1$
2. Countable additivity: For disjoint $E_i$, $P(\bigcup E_i) = \sum P(E_i)$

**Conditional Probability**: $P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$ for $P(B) > 0$

**Independence**: $A$ and $B$ independent iff $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$

**Law of Total Probability**: 
If $\{B_i\}$ partition $\Omega$, then $P(A) = \sum_i P(A|B_i)P(B_i)$

**Bayes' Theorem**:
$P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{\sum_i P(B|A_i)P(A_i)}$

**Expectation**: For random variable $X$,
$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x P(X=x)$ (discrete)
$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int x f(x) dx$ (continuous)

**Variance**: $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$

**Standard Deviation**: $\sigma_X = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$

### 1.6 Dirac Notation (Bra-Ket)

**Ket**: $|\psi\rangle$ represents a vector in Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$.

**Bra**: $\langle\phi|$ represents a linear functional (dual vector), specifically $\langle\phi| = |\phi\rangle^\dagger$.

**Inner Product**: $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$ gives complex number representing "overlap".

**Outer Product**: $|\psi\rangle\langle\phi|$ gives linear operator (matrix).

**Tensor Product**: $|\psi\rangle \otimes |\phi\rangle = |\psi\rangle|\phi\rangle = |\psi\phi\rangle$

**Expectation Value**: For observable $A$ (Hermitian operator) in state $|\psi\rangle$:
$\langle A \rangle = \langle\psi|A|\psi\rangle$

**Probability**: Probability of measuring eigenvalue $\lambda$ corresponding to eigenvector $|\phi\rangle$ in state $|\psi\rangle$ is $|\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2$.

### Summary of Mathematical Prerequisites

| Concept | Why It's Needed | Key Formulas |
|---------|----------------|--------------|
| Complex Numbers | Quantum amplitudes are complex | $z = re^{i\theta}$, $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ |
| Linear Algebra | State vectors, operators, evolution | $|\psi'\rangle = U|\psi\rangle$, $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1$ |
| Inner Product | Measurement probabilities, orthogonality | $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$, $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle = 0$ |
| Eigenvalues/Eigenvectors | Measurement outcomes, observable spectrum | $A|v\rangle = \lambda|v\rangle$ |
| Tensor Product | Multi-qubit systems, entanglement | $|0\rangle\otimes|1\rangle = |01\rangle$ |
| Probability | Measurement statistics, Born rule | $P(i) = |\langle i|\psi\rangle|^2$ |

### Practice Problems

**Problem 1.1**: Normalize the vector $\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4i \end{bmatrix}$.

**Problem 1.2**: Show that the Pauli matrices are both Hermitian and unitary.

**Problem 1.3**: For $z = 1 + i\sqrt{3}$, find $z^3$ using polar form.

**Problem 1.4**: Prove that the set of all $n \times n$ unitary matrices forms a group.

**Problem 1.5**: Given $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$, find its eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

**Problem 1.6**: If $|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}|0\rangle + \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}|1\rangle$, what is the probability of measuring $|0\rangle$?

**Problem 1.7**: Show that the tensor product of two orthonormal bases is orthonormal.

**Problem 1.8**: For operator $A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$, find eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

### Solutions

**Solution 1.1**: 
Norm: $\sqrt{|3|^2 + |4i|^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$
Normalized: $\frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4i \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3/5 \\ 4i/5 \end{bmatrix}$

**Solution 1.2**: 
Pauli-X: $X = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$
$X^\dagger = X^T = X$ (Hermitian)
$X^2 = I$ (Unitary)
Similar for Y and Z.

**Solution 1.3**: 
$z = 1 + i\sqrt{3} = 2e^{i\pi/3}$
$z^3 = 2^3 e^{i\pi} = 8(-1) = -8$

**Solution 1.4**: 
- Closure: $(UV)^\dagger(UV) = V^\dagger U^\dagger UV = I$
- Associativity: Inherited
- Identity: $I$ is unitary
- Inverse: $U^{-1} = U^\dagger$ is unitary

**Solution 1.5**: 
$\det(A-\lambda I) = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$
$\lambda = 1, 3$
For $\lambda=1$: $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}=0 \Rightarrow x+y=0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix}$
For $\lambda=3$: $\begin{bmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}=0 \Rightarrow -x+y=0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$

**Solution 1.6**: 
$P(0) = |\langle 0|\psi\rangle|^2 = \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{3}$

**Solution 1.7**: 
Let $\{|v_i\rangle\}$ and $\{|w_j\rangle\}$ be orthonormal bases.
Then $\langle v_i\otimes w_j | v_k\otimes w_l \rangle = (\langle v_i|v_k\rangle)(\langle w_j|w_l\rangle) = \delta_{ik}\delta_{jl}$
Thus orthonormal.

**Solution 1.8**: 
$\det(A-\lambda I) = \lambda^2 - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = \pm 1$
For $\lambda=1$: $\begin{bmatrix}-1&-i\\i&-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}=0 \Rightarrow -x-iy=0, ix-y=0 \Rightarrow y=ix \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\i\end{bmatrix}$
For $\lambda=-1$: $\begin{bmatrix}1&-i\\i&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}=0 \Rightarrow x-iy=0, ix+y=0 \Rightarrow y=-ix \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\-i\end{bmatrix}$
